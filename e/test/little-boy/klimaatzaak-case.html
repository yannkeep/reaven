Klimaatzaak: Belgium Was Ordered to Act on Climate

The court ruled. Belgium ignored it. Another chapter in rule of law breakdown.

In June 2021, a Brussels court delivered a landmark ruling in Klimaatzaak v. Belgium: the state was ordered to reduce greenhouse gas emissions in line with European and international commitments. The ruling was celebrated as a victory for climate litigation.

Years later, Belgium remains in non-compliance. The pattern mirrors the Fedasil crisis: court orders become suggestions, fines become operational costs, and rule of law erodes.

The Ruling (June 2021)

The Court ordered Belgium to reduce emissions by at least 55% by 2030 compared to 1990 levels, in line with IPCC recommendations and European commitments.

The ruling was binding on all Belgian governments: federal, regional, and community.

The Pattern of Non-Compliance

Klimaatzaak follows the same script as Fedasil:

Stage 1: Symbolic acceptance. Government expresses respect for the ruling.

Stage 2: Procedural delays. Coordination between federal and regional levels creates bureaucratic paralysis.

Stage 3: Insufficient action. Measures announced fall short of requirements.

Stage 4: Threatened fines. Plaintiffs return to court seeking enforcement mechanisms.

Stage 5: Fines as cost of doing business. State calculates that paying penalties is politically easier than transformative action.

"The state treats judicial rulings as negotiating positions rather than binding obligations. This fundamentally undermines the architecture of law."

Why It Matters Beyond Climate

Implications for Rule of Law

  • Demonstrates that court victories may be pyrrhic — winning in court doesn't guarantee compliance
  • Creates precedent: if the state ignores climate rulings, what rulings will it obey?
  • Erodes public trust in judiciary as effective check on government
  • Shifts burden to citizens to continuously litigate for basic compliance
  • Shows particracy shields executive from accountability across all domains

The Coordination Excuse

Belgium's response often cites the complexity of coordinating between federal and regional governments. This is technically true — Belgium's institutional architecture is labyrinthine.

But it's also an excuse. Other federal states (Germany, Switzerland, Austria) manage climate policy coordination. The Belgian system's fragmentation is a feature, not a bug — it enables responsibility diffusion where everyone is competent but no one is accountable.

Climate + Social Crisis = Convergence

The Arizona budget analysis shows Belgium projecting climate impacts of 2.8-5% GDP by 2050. Meanwhile, the social safety net is being dismantled precisely when climate shocks will intensify.

Klimaatzaak isn't just about emissions. It's about a state's capacity to respond to existential challenges. A state that cannot comply with court orders on climate cannot be trusted to protect its population from climate consequences.

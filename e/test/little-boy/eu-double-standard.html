Rule of Law Double Standard?

Belgium ignores 9,000 court orders. Hungary faces Article 7. Why the difference?

The European Union has positioned itself as guardian of rule of law. Proceedings against Hungary and Poland dominate headlines. But what happens when a founding member ‚Äî one hosting EU institutions ‚Äî demonstrates systematic non-compliance with court rulings?

This investigation found no evidence of formal EU proceedings against Belgium for its documented rule of law failures. The question is: why not?

The Uncomfortable Question

If systematic non-compliance with 9,000+ court orders occurred in Hungary, would the EU's response be the same silence?

Possible Explanations

Why the Different Treatment?

  1. Institutional proximity: Belgium hosts EU institutions. Investigating the host country creates awkward dynamics.
  2. Coalition politics: Belgian political families (EPP, S&D, Renew) are well-represented in EU structures. Hungary's Fidesz was expelled from EPP.
  3. Visibility asymmetry: Hungary's rule of law issues receive massive media attention. Belgium's fly under the radar.
  4. Nature of violations: Hungary's violations are characterized as "systemic attacks on democracy." Belgium's are framed as "administrative failures."
  5. Founding member deference: Unwritten norm that founding members receive gentler treatment.

The Credibility Cost

Why This Matters for Europe

When the EU enforces rule of law selectively, it undermines its own legitimacy. Critics in Hungary and Poland can point to Belgium and ask: "Why us and not them?"

The double standard doesn't just affect Belgium. It weakens the entire European project of shared values and equal treatment under law.

What Needs to Change

This isn't an argument against EU action on Hungary or Poland. Their violations are real and documented. This is an argument for consistency.

Rule of law either applies to everyone or it applies to no one. A founding member systematically ignoring court orders is a rule of law crisis ‚Äî regardless of geography or institutional convenience.

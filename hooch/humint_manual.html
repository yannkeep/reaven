Overview of HUMINT Operations

Human Intelligence (HUMINT) is intelligence derived from information collected and provided by human sources. It is the oldest discipline of intelligence collection, predating all technical means, and remains the only reliable method for determining an adversary's intentions, plans, and decision-making processes.

The Intelligence Cycle Applied to HUMINT

The HUMINT collection cycle follows the standard intelligence cycle with adaptations for human-source operations. Planning and Direction translates policy-maker requirements into Priority Intelligence Requirements (PIRs). Collection involves the operational contact with sources — the core of HUMINT work. Processing evaluates source reliability and information credibility. Analysis integrates HUMINT with other intelligence disciplines. Dissemination delivers finished intelligence while protecting sources and methods.

COUNTERINTELLIGENCE WARNING

Walk-ins and volunteers must always be treated as potential adversary operations (dangles) until thoroughly vetted. The KGB and MSS have historically used walk-in operations to penetrate Western intelligence services.

Tradecraft Fundamentals

Tradecraft encompasses the techniques and procedures used to conduct HUMINT operations securely. Key elements include cover management (maintaining a believable reason for being in a location or meeting a person), surveillance detection routes (SDRs), dead drops and live drops, brush contacts, signalling systems, and secure communications protocols.

The Agent Meeting

The agent meeting is the fundamental unit of HUMINT operations. It typically involves a pre-meeting counter-surveillance phase, a meeting phase (debriefing, tasking, communication), and a post-meeting phase (report writing, intelligence processing). Meeting locations must be pre-surveyed, offer natural cover for the encounter, have multiple entry and exit routes, and be free from hostile surveillance coverage.

Debriefing Techniques

Effective debriefing is both an art and a science. The debriefer must establish rapport, use open-ended questions to elicit maximum information, employ cognitive interview techniques for memory enhancement, probe for inconsistencies without antagonizing the source, and document responses with sufficient detail for analyst exploitation. The PEACE model (Preparation, Engage, Account, Closure, Evaluate) provides a structured framework.

Counterintelligence Considerations

Every HUMINT operation carries counterintelligence risks. Sources may be doubled, fabricating intelligence, or operating under hostile control. Indicators of compromise include unexplained changes in a source's lifestyle, inconsistencies in reporting, intelligence that is too good or too convenient, failure to pass polygraph examinations, and changes in communication patterns.

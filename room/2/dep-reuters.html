Belgium to cut 90,000 from unemployment benefits in biggest welfare overhaul since 1944

BRUSSELS, Feb 3 (Reuters)

Belgium's Arizona coalition government is set to exclude approximately 90,000 long-term unemployed people from the national unemployment insurance system by end-2027, in what unions call the country's biggest social policy overhaul since the establishment of its welfare state in 1944.

The reform, which limits unemployment benefits to 24 months, will take effect in waves starting March 1, 2026, according to data from the National Employment Office (ONEM/RVA).

Belgium was the last country in Western Europe to maintain unlimited unemployment benefits, a feature enshrined in Article 23 of its constitution, which includes a "standstill" clause prohibiting significant regression in social protection.

A constitutional challenge was filed on Oct. 29, 2025 by the country's major trade unions — FGTB, CSC, and CGSLB, which together represent 1.5 million members — along with the Belgian League for Human Rights. A ruling is expected in late 2026 or early 2027.

The reform will redirect excluded individuals to municipal social assistance centres (CPAS/OCMW), with a federal compensation of 518 euros per person per year. Municipal federations have called the amount "derisory" and estimated the real cost at between 15,000 and 22,000 euros per person annually.

Prime Minister Bart De Wever of the Flemish nationalist N-VA party has defended the reform as necessary to reduce Belgium's 21 billion euro annual passive spending on unemployment and disability benefits.

Critics point to Britain's Work Capability Assessment, a similar scheme that was linked to 590 additional suicides between 2010-2013 and is now being phased out.

Brussels, which accounts for 10% of Belgium's population but 32% of projected exclusions, faces an estimated 80 million euro annual cost increase for its 19 municipal social assistance centres.

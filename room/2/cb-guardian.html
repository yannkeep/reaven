Belgium is about to repeat Britain's worst welfare mistake

We documented 590 additional suicides caused by the Work Capability Assessment. Now Belgium wants to copy the model we're abolishing. We have a responsibility to warn them.
DB
Dr. Ben Barr & Prof. David Stuckler
University of Liverpool / Bocconi University — Public health researchers

In 2015, we published a study in the Journal of Epidemiology and Community Health that changed the British welfare debate. Our research documented that the Work Capability Assessment — the UK government's mechanism for reassessing disability and long-term unemployment benefits — was associated with 590 additional suicides, 279,000 additional cases of mental health problems, and 725,000 additional antidepressant prescriptions between 2010 and 2013.

These numbers were not contested by the government. They were confirmed by subsequent studies. In November 2023, the UK government announced the WCA would be abolished. In March 2025, it confirmed the timeline.

We are writing this piece because Belgium — a country whose social security system we have long admired — is about to implement a remarkably similar mechanism. The Arizona coalition government, led by Flemish nationalist Bart De Wever, has voted to limit unemployment benefits to 24 months, effective 2026. Approximately 90,000 people will lose their benefits and be redirected to municipal social assistance (CPAS), with drastically reduced support and intrusive means-testing.

The parallels with the WCA are striking. Both target long-term benefit recipients. Both assume that withdrawal of support will produce "activation." Both transfer costs to local authorities without adequate compensation. Both disproportionately affect older workers with health conditions. And both ignore the epidemiological evidence that benefit sanctions cause illness, not employment.

The Belgian context contains additional risk factors. The country is implementing simultaneous austerity measures on long-term sick leave (-2.5% penalty), medication reimbursement (€26.2 million in cuts), and the right to protest (new "anti-rioter" legislation). This combination creates a perfect storm of social stress with no institutional safety valve.

The Belgian government's argument is that their system differs from the WCA because it does not involve medical reassessment — only a time limit. This is a distinction without a difference. The pathological mechanism is the same: the sudden removal of economic security from vulnerable people who lack realistic alternatives. Whether you cut someone's benefits because a computer says they're "fit for work" or because a calendar says they've been unemployed for 25 months, the psychological impact is indistinguishable.

Belgium has a constitutional safeguard that the UK lacks: Article 23 of the Belgian Constitution includes a "standstill" principle that prohibits significant regression in social protection without proportionate justification. A constitutional challenge was filed on 29 October 2025. We can only hope the Belgian Constitutional Court takes the epidemiological evidence into account.

We spent fifteen years documenting what benefit sanctions do to human beings. We have a professional and moral obligation to warn any country that proposes to repeat the experiment. Belgium: we measured the cost of this policy in British lives. Please do not force us to measure it in Belgian ones.

Ben Barr is Professor of Applied Public Health Research at the University of Liverpool. David Stuckler is Professor of Political Economy and Sociology at Bocconi University.

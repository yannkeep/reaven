Belgium's Arizona gamble: when austerity meets constitutional rights

The country that invented European social security is dismantling it — and the Constitutional Court may be the last line of defense.
RP
Ramón Peña-Casas
European Social Observatory (OSE) — Senior researcher

Belgium's Arizona coalition has a problem that no other European government currently faces: a constitutional provision that explicitly prohibits regression in social protection. Article 23 of the Belgian Constitution enshrines the right to social security with a "standstill" clause — the level of protection cannot decrease without proportionate justification.

This makes Belgium a unique laboratory for a question that concerns the entire European Union: can austerity override constitutional social rights?

The test case is the limitation of unemployment benefits to 24 months, which will affect approximately 90,000 people starting in 2026. The measure transfers beneficiaries from federal unemployment insurance to municipal social assistance (CPAS/OCMW), with a federal compensation of €518 per person per year — a fraction of the actual cost.

The constitutional challenge, filed on 29 October 2025 by Belgium's trade union front, the Human Rights League, and several civil society organisations, argues three points: the reduction is "significant" within the meaning of Article 23; the justification is not proportionate (alternatives exist, including tax reform targeting Belgium's exceptionally low capital taxation); and the compensation to municipalities is inadequate.

The European dimension is critical. Belgium's Article 23 standstill is one of the strongest constitutional social protections in the EU. If the Constitutional Court upholds the Arizona reforms, it will signal that even the most robust constitutional frameworks cannot resist the austerity consensus. If it strikes them down, it will demonstrate that social rights have teeth — a precedent that could resonate from Lisbon to Tallinn.

The European Committee of Social Rights, which monitors compliance with the European Social Charter, has already flagged comparable regressions in other member states. The Belgian case could become a landmark.

Meanwhile, the Arizona coalition is simultaneously implementing measures that restrict the right to protest (the "anti-rioter" law), threaten civic associations with administrative dissolution (the "Quintin law," blocked by the Council of State in January 2026), and penalise long-term sick workers. The convergence of social regression and democratic restriction is not unique to Belgium — but Belgium is where the constitutional showdown will happen first.

Europe should be watching.

Ramón Peña-Casas is a senior researcher at the European Social Observatory (OSE) in Brussels, specialising in poverty, social inclusion, and European social policy.
